The Dirty Dozen

The ‘dirty dozen’ chemicals are the chemicals of greatest concern as outlined in the Stockholm Convention. These chemicals were singled out for their high past use in industry.

Action to ban the use of Chlordane has been taken in Austria, Belgium, Bolivia, Brazil, Chile, Columbia, Costa, Rica, Denmark, Dominican Republic, EU, Kenya, Korea, Lebanon, Liechtenstein, Mozambique, Netherlands, Norway, Panama, Paraguay, Philippines, Poland, Portugal, Santa Lucia, Singapore, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, Tonga, Turkey, United Kingdom, Yemen, and Yugoslavia

Its use is severely restricted or limited to non-agricultural uses in Argentina, Belize, Bulgaria, Canada, China, Cyprus, Dominica, Egypt, Honduras, Indonesia, Israel, Mexico, New Zealand, South Africa, Sri Lanka, USA, and Venezuela

EPA guidelines for drinking water: no more than 60 ppb chlordane should be present in drinking water that children consume for no longer than 10 days. Drinking water should contain no more than 0.5 ppb for children or 2 ppb for adults if they drink the water for longer periods.

EPA stopped all use of chlordane on food crops, effective March 1978

Until 1988, EPA permitted chlordane use for termite control or dipping the roots or tops of nonfood plants.

On April 14, 1988, however, EPA stopped all sales and commercial use of chlordane.

The FDA established that the levels of chlordane and its breakdown products in most fruits and vegetables should not be greater than 300 ppb and in animal fat and fish should not be greater than 100 ppb.

Federal regulations limit the amount of chlordane that factories can release into waste water.

The EPA requires industry to report releases or spills of 1 pound or more. A temporary guideline of the National Research Council indicated that 0.005 mg/m³ should be the maximum amount allowed in the air of military housing.

The Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) regulates chlordane levels in the workplace. The maximum allowable level in workplace air is 0.5 mg/m³ for a person who is exposed for 8 hours per workday and 40 hours per workweek.

The National Institute for Occupational Health and Safety (NIOSH) also recommends an exposure limit of 5 mg/m³ for a person exposed to chlordane in the workplace for 8 hours per workday and 40 hours per workweek.

Viscous, amber colored liquid

Chlordane also affects neuron channels in the body. Specifically, chlordane acts as an inhibitor to gamma-aminobutyric acid, which is a neurotransmitter blocking brain signals [8]. The chloride ions in chlordane increase concentration of chloride ions, decreasing the chloride intake to cells. This prevents brain signals from being stopped. Additionally, chlordane also interferes with the gap junction communication between cells by accumulating on the lipid bilayers of cell membranes, hence interfering with cell-cell communication [9]. This is most likely attributed to chlordane’s lipophilic nature.

During its use, DDT primarily inhibited the closure of sodium ion channels, meaning that muscle signals only can fire. DDT’s structure allows it to bind to the sodium ion channels, allowing them to fire longer, and depolarizing the membrane. Similar to chlordane, DDT also interferes with intercellular communication by interacting with the lipophilic bilayer of cells, disrupting gap junction interaction between cells. This likely promotes cancer.

Insecticide for fire ants

Main route of human exposure to mirex is through food: particularly meat, fish, and wild game.

EPA suggests that ingesting an amount of mirex equal to 200 picograms (pg) per kilogram (kg) of your body weight per day is not likely to cause significant harmful health effects

In general, this causes decreased membrane permeability, leading to decreased biliary excretion.

Entering the body, PCBs are lipophilic, and absorbed by the digestive tract and then filtered by the liver and kidneys. PCBs cause damage to the nervous system, digestive tract, liver, and kidneys. PCBs also affect the nervous system by disrupting calcium ion channels. This generally occurs among PCB mixtures with higher chlorination, as the chemical can rotate to interact more effectively with the receptors. This generally inhibits nervous system signals [17].

It tends to affect this region of the cell because of the changing concentration of chlorine, which toxaphene disrupts. Additionally, toxaphene may disrupt ATPase by interacting with the proton pump, as a result of toxaphene’s tendencies to remain near the plasma membrane [15]. Although toxaphene has been shown to induce apoptosis and production of free radicals, it is not entirely clear how it affects the immune system.
