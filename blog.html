The Dirty Dozen

The ‘dirty dozen’ chemicals are the chemicals of greatest concern as outlined in the Stockholm Convention. These chemicals were singled out for their high past use in industry.

Aldrin

C12H8Cl6O

Aldrin is an intentionally produced chemical which is most commonly found in pesticides

  • Emissions
  • Dosage
  • Management/Remediation
  • Chemical Properties

Used to control soil insects such as termites, corn rootworm, wireworms, rice water weevil, and grasshoppers.

Used to protect crops (corn+potatoes)

Effective to protect wooden structures from termites

Dairy products, such as milk and butter, and animal meats are the primary sources of exposure

Lethal dose for adult man ≈ 5g

The average daily human intake of Aldrin and Dieldrin in India is calculated to be 19µg/person

average daily human intake of Aldrin and Dieldrin in Vietnam = calculated to be 0.55 µg/person

Banned in many countries, including Bulgaria, Ecuador, Finland, Hungary, Israel, Singapore, Switzerland, and Turkey

Severely restricted in many countries, including Argentina, Austria, Canada, Chile, the EU, Japan, New Zealand, the Philippines, USA, and Venezuela.

The current permissible workplace exposure limit for aldrin and dieldrin enforced by the Occupational Safety and Health Administration is 0.25 mg/m3 (OSHA, 1981)

The ACGIH (1981) has recommended for aldrin and dieldrin a TLV-TWA of 0.25 mg/m3 and a TLV-STEL of 0.75 mg/m3

Both agencies noted that aldrin and dieldrin are absorbed through the skin and that dermal exposure should therefore be avoided.

Aldrin is a white crystalline solid which is difficult to break down

Half-life of approximately 369 days in blood

Toxic Effects On...

  • Humans
  • Plants
  • Animals

Signs of symptoms of intoxication include headache, dizziness, nausea, general malaise and vomiting, followed by muscle twitching, myoclonic jerks, and convulsions

The EPA (1979c) has estimated that exposure of aldrin and dieldrin in the drinking water at concentrations of 0.0046 and 0.0044 ng/L, respectively, would result in a lifetime cancer risk of 10−6

Low phytotoxicity with plants affected only by extremely high application rates

Acutely toxic to laboratory animals by the oral, dermal, and inhalation routes

Carcinogenic in mice

At 6 mg/kg there was 41 percent mortality in rats and increased liver-to-body weight ratios and decreased weight gain in mice

Chlordane

C12H8Cl6O

Aldrin is an intentionally produced chemical which is most commonly found in pesticides

  • Emissions
  • Dosage
  • Management/Remediation
  • Chemical Properties

exposure may occur through food but, due to its highly restricted uses, this route does not appear to be a major pathway of exposure.

stays in the environment for many years and is still found in food, air, water, and soil + accumulate in fish, birds, and mammals

still commonly found in some form in the fat of fish, birds, mammals, and almost all humans.

Most common source of exposure is from ingesting - contaminated food. remains in the food supply because much of the farmland was treated with chlordane in the 1960s and 1970s, and it remains in some soil for over 20 years

Action to ban the use of Chlordane has been taken in Austria, Belgium, Bolivia, Brazil, Chile, Columbia, Costa, Rica, Denmark, Dominican Republic, EU, Kenya, Korea, Lebanon, Liechtenstein, Mozambique, Netherlands, Norway, Panama, Paraguay, Philippines, Poland, Portugal, Santa Lucia, Singapore, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, Tonga, Turkey, United Kingdom, Yemen, and Yugoslavia

Its use is severely restricted or limited to non-agricultural uses in Argentina, Belize, Bulgaria, Canada, China, Cyprus, Dominica, Egypt, Honduras, Indonesia, Israel, Mexico, New Zealand, South Africa, Sri Lanka, USA, and Venezuela

EPA guidelines for drinking water: no more than 60 ppb chlordane should be present in drinking water that children consume for no longer than 10 days. Drinking water should contain no more than 0.5 ppb for children or 2 ppb for adults if they drink the water for longer periods.

EPA stopped all use of chlordane on food crops, effective March 1978

Until 1988, EPA permitted chlordane use for termite control or dipping the roots or tops of nonfood plants.

On April 14, 1988, however, EPA stopped all sales and commercial use of chlordane.

The FDA established that the levels of chlordane and its breakdown products in most fruits and vegetables should not be greater than 300 ppb and in animal fat and fish should not be greater than 100 ppb.

Federal regulations limit the amount of chlordane that factories can release into waste water.

The EPA requires industry to report releases or spills of 1 pound or more. A temporary guideline of the National Research Council indicated that 0.005 mg/m³ should be the maximum amount allowed in the air of military housing.

The Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) regulates chlordane levels in the workplace. The maximum allowable level in workplace air is 0.5 mg/m³ for a person who is exposed for 8 hours per workday and 40 hours per workweek.

The National Institute for Occupational Health and Safety (NIOSH) also recommends an exposure limit of 5 mg/m³ for a person exposed to chlordane in the workplace for 8 hours per workday and 40 hours per workweek.

Viscous, amber colored liquid

Chlordane also affects neuron channels in the body. Specifically, chlordane acts as an inhibitor to gamma-aminobutyric acid, which is a neurotransmitter blocking brain signals [8]. The chloride ions in chlordane increase concentration of chloride ions, decreasing the chloride intake to cells. This prevents brain signals from being stopped. Additionally, chlordane also interferes with the gap junction communication between cells by accumulating on the lipid bilayers of cell membranes, hence interfering with cell-cell communication [9]. This is most likely attributed to chlordane’s lipophilic nature.

Significant changes in immune system reported

Chlordane exposure has not been associated with increased risk of mortality from cancer

Large amounts of chlordane taken by mouth can cause convulsions and death

Swallowing small amounts/breathing air containing high concentrations can cause headaches, irritation, confusion, weakness, and vision problems, as well as upset stomach, vomiting, stomach cramps, diarrhea, and jaundice.

One study found minor changes in liver function in workers in Japan who used chlordane as a pesticide

Animals given high levels of chlordane by mouth for short periods of time died or had convulsions

Long-term exposure of animals to chlordane in their food caused harmful effects in the liver

DDT

C12H8Cl6O

Aldrin is an intentionally produced chemical which is most commonly found in pesticides

  • Dosage

DDT is still used to control mosquito vectors of malaria in numerous countries

Used on humans to battle typhus and malaria

Persistent in environment; 50% can remain in soil after 10-15 years

High accumulation of DDT at higher trophic levels in aquatic systems

Detected in food from all over the world

Animal and fatty food contain levels of DDT (stored in fat)

While adults appear to tolerate moderate to high ingested doses of up to 280 mg/kg, a case of fatal poisoning was seen in a child who ingested one ounce of a 5% DDT:kerosene solution

Use of DDT has been banned in 34 countries and severely restricted in 34 other countries

The countries that have banned DDT include Argentina, Australia, Bulgaria, Canada, Colombia,Cyprus, Ethiopia, Finland, Hong Kong, Japan, Lebanon, Mozambique, Norway, Switzerland, and the USA

Countries that have severely restricted its use include Belize, Ecuador, the EU, India, Israel, Kenya, Mexico, Panama, and Thailand.

“When preparing fish, remove the skin, trim the fat, and broil, bake, or grill the fish so that the fat drips away”

Choose lean meat cuts, buy organic meats, and limit dairy fat

In the body, many metabolites of DDT, including DDE, which is the result of DDT losing hydrogen chloride, are released. During its use, DDT primarily inhibited the closure of sodium ion channels, meaning that muscle signals only can fire. DDT’s structure allows it to bind to the sodium ion channels, allowing them to fire longer, and depolarizing the membrane. Similar to chlordane, DDT also interferes with intercellular communication by interacting with the lipophilic bilayer of cells, disrupting gap junction interaction between cells. This likely promotes cancer.

  • Plants
  • Animals

“Studies involving human volunteers who ingested DDT for up to 21 months did not result in any observed adverse effects”

Possible risk of breast cancer

Some evidence suggesting its suppressive to immune system

Exposure harmful to nervous system

Exposed to high levels - dizziness, tremor, irritability, convulsions

Workers with long term exposure experience neurological and cognitive problems

Pregnant women exposed = premature/small-for-gestational age babies likely

Hormone disrupting chemical due to estrogen-like properties

DDT is a possible human carcinogen, or cancer-causing agent

Causes cancer in lab animals

Not acutely tonic to lab animals

In study of mice, no effect on fertility, gestation, viability, lactation, or survival were observed

Highly toxic to fish as it affects fish behavior and temperature selection: "“Atlantic salmon exposed to DDT as eggs experienced impaired balance and delayed appearance of normal behaviour patterns.”

Acutely toxic to birds; birds of prey most susceptible; showed extensive egg shell thinning in wild

Concern of effect on environment (especially wild birds) caused restrictions/bans in many developed countries in early 1970s.

Aldrin

C12H8Cl6O

Aldrin is an intentionally produced chemical which is most commonly found in pesticides

  • Dosage

Used to control soil insects such as termites, corn rootworm, wireworms, rice water weevil, and grasshoppers.

Used to protect crops (corn+potatoes)

Effective to protect wooden structures from termites

Dairy products, such as milk and butter, and animal meats are the primary sources of exposure

Lethal dose for adult man ≈ 5g

The average daily human intake of Aldrin and Dieldrin in India is calculated to be 19µg/person

average daily human intake of Aldrin and Dieldrin in Vietnam = calculated to be 0.55 µg/person

Banned in many countries, including Bulgaria, Ecuador, Finland, Hungary, Israel, Singapore, Switzerland, and Turkey

Severely restricted in many countries, including Argentina, Austria, Canada, Chile, the EU, Japan, New Zealand, the Philippines, USA, and Venezuela.

The current permissible workplace exposure limit for aldrin and dieldrin enforced by the Occupational Safety and Health Administration is 0.25 mg/m3 (OSHA, 1981)

The ACGIH (1981) has recommended for aldrin and dieldrin a TLV-TWA of 0.25 mg/m3 and a TLV-STEL of 0.75 mg/m3

Both agencies noted that aldrin and dieldrin are absorbed through the skin and that dermal exposure should therefore be avoided.

Aldrin is a white crystalline solid which is difficult to break down

Half-life of approximately 369 days in blood

  • Plants
  • Animals

Signs of symptoms of intoxication include headache, dizziness, nausea, general malaise and vomiting, followed by muscle twitching, myoclonic jerks, and convulsions

Acutely toxic to laboratory animals by the oral, dermal, and inhalation routes

Carcinogenic in mice
