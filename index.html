What is a Persistent Organic Pollutant?

  • Overview
  • StockHolm Convention
  • Lifespan

A POP (Persistent Organic Pollutant) are synthetically produced chemicals that have toxic effects on our body. Many of these effects were previously unknown when the chemicals were produced.

The Stockholm Convention defines these as “chemical substances that persist in the environment, bio-accumulate through the food web, and pose a risk of causing adverse effects to human health and environment”

These pollutants are resistant to breakdown, and hence are present long after their production. This means that they can accumulate over time in the human body, spreading across atmospheres and oceans.

What are some sources of POPs?

  • Industrial Use
  • Food Chains
  • Wild Animals
  • Marine Systems
  • Direct Contact
  • Water

POPs were used commonly in industries such as disease control and agriculture. At the time, these manufactured chemicals had great purpose, but were later found to cause significant damage to humans and the environment.

Because of the ability for POPs to remain in the environment, they have accumulated in many food chains (which eventually passes down to us). As such, the most common source of toxic POPs are contaminated foods.

Food with higher amounts of POP typically comes from fish, shellfish, or other wild animals with high fat content. This is because many of the POPs are lipophilic, tending to remain and dissolve inside fat.

POPs also deposit in marine systems as a result of rain, runoff, and waste discharge, making aquatic systems a particular reservoir for POPs. Additionally, because of their low water solubility, they become particulates, forming sediments. While POPs are largely benign in this state, they become toxic when reintroduced into system circulation.

Although less common, direct contact with POP is also possible, such as touching a contaminated object, or using an item manufactured in a POP-contaminated factory.

POPs can be uncommonly found in contaminated water.
